
# Cumulative Analysis

## Objective

The objective of this analysis is to evaluate how revenue, orders, quantities, and customer value accumulate over time.



# 1. Monthly Cumulative Business Performance

## Business Question

How do revenue, orders, and quantities accumulate over time?

## SQL Concepts Used

* Window Functions
* Running Totals
* SUM() OVER()
* Time-Series Analysis

## Results

| Metric              | Value   |
| ------------------- | ------- |
| Total Revenue       | $29.35M |
| Total Orders        | 27,657  |
| Total Quantity Sold | 60,404  |

Key milestones:

| Date     | Cumulative Revenue |
| -------- | ------------------ |
| Dec 2011 | $7.12M             |
| Dec 2012 | $12.96M            |
| Dec 2013 | $29.31M            |

## Insights

* Revenue growth accelerated significantly during 2013.
* The company generated more revenue in 2013 than in the previous two years combined.
* Nearly all business value was accumulated before January 2014.
* January 2014 contains only a partial month and should not be used for trend evaluation.

# 4. Customer Lifetime Revenue Tracking

## Business Question

How does customer value accumulate throughout the customer lifecycle?

## SQL Concepts Used

* Running Totals by Customer
* ROW_NUMBER()
* Customer Journey Analysis
* Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)

## Example Output

For each customer:

* Order sequence is tracked chronologically.
* Cumulative revenue grows after each purchase.
* Full purchase history can be reconstructed.

Example:

| Customer | Order Sequence | Cumulative Revenue |
| -------- | -------------- | ------------------ |
| 2141     | 10             | $325               |
| 4469     | 10             | $437               |
| 1497     | 4              | $205               |

## Insights

* Customer revenue accumulation is highly uneven.
* Many customers place only a few low-value orders.
* Other customers continue purchasing over multiple years and generate substantially higher lifetime value.
* This structure supports CLV modeling and customer retention initiatives.

# 6. Revenue Concentration by Customer Segment

## Business Question

How concentrated is revenue among customers?

## SQL Concepts Used

* Pareto Analysis
* Running Revenue Contribution
* Customer Segmentation
* Window Functions

## Segmentation Logic

| Segment             | Definition                      |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| VIP Customers       | First 80% of cumulative revenue |
| Mid Customers       | 80% - 95% cumulative revenue    |
| Low Value Customers | Remaining customers             |

## Results

| Segment             | Customers | Percentage |
| ------------------- | --------- | ---------- |
| Low Value Customers | 10,739    | 58.10%     |
| VIP Customers       | 5,149     | 27.86%     |
| Mid Customers       | 2,596     | 14.04%     |

## Insights

* Revenue is highly concentrated among a relatively small customer group.
* Only 27.86% of customers belong to the VIP segment.
* More than half of customers generate only a small fraction of total revenue.
* The business exhibits a classic Pareto-style revenue distribution.
* Retaining VIP customers should be a strategic priority due to their disproportionate revenue contribution.

---

# Executive Summary

The cumulative analysis reveals a business that experienced rapid expansion during 2013 and generated a total revenue of approximately **$29.35M**.

Key findings include:

* Revenue accumulation accelerated significantly in 2013.
* More than **80% of total revenue** was accumulated by September 2013.
* Growth momentum showed multiple acceleration and slowdown cycles, indicating dynamic market conditions.
* Customer value is highly concentrated among a relatively small group of customers.
* **5,149 VIP customers (27.86%) drive the majority of business revenue**, confirming a strong Pareto distribution.
* Customer retention and VIP customer management represent the highest-impact opportunities for future revenue growth.

> **Data Quality Note:** December 2010 and January 2014 are partial periods within the dataset. These months were included in cumulative calculations but should not be used for evaluating long-term growth trends.
